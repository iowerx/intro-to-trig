    Chapter 1: Geometric Foundations

    Introduction

    Welcome to the study of trigonometric functions! Before we dive into trigonometry, we need to establish a solid foundation in geometry. This chapter reviews essential geometric concepts that form the building blocks for understanding trigonometric ratios.

    Geometry provides the visual and logical framework for trigonometry. When you understand triangles—their properties, angles, and relationships—you'll be well-prepared to explore how trigonometric functions connect angles to side lengths.

    Chapter Learning Objectives

    After completing this chapter, you will be able to:

    • Remember: Recall definitions of different triangle types and angle types
    • Understand: Explain the properties of triangles and angle relationships
    • Apply: Use angle properties to solve geometric problems
    • Analyze: Compare different triangle classifications and identify key features

    Section 1.1: Angle Fundamentals

    What is an Angle?

    An angle is formed by two rays that share a common endpoint (called the vertex). Angles measure the amount of rotation from one ray to the other, typically measured in degrees.

    Angle Notation: An angle can be labeled with three letters (with the vertex in the middle) or with a single letter or number representing the vertex.

    Angle Types

    Acute Angle - Measures less than 90° - Sharp or pointed appearance - Examples: 30°, 45°, 60°, 80°

    Right Angle - Measures exactly 90° - Shown with a small square symbol in diagrams - Created when two perpendicular lines meet - Example: Corners of a square or rectangle

    Obtuse Angle - Measures greater than 90° but less than 180° - Wide or spread-out appearance - Examples: 100°, 120°, 150°

    Straight Angle - Measures exactly 180° - Forms a straight line - Actually two rays pointing in opposite directions

    Angle Relationships

    Complementary Angles - Two angles that sum to exactly 90° - Example: 30° and 60° are complementary - In a right triangle, the two acute angles are always complementary - If one angle is x°, its complement is (90 - x)°

    Supplementary Angles - Two angles that sum to exactly 180° - Example: 120° and 60° are supplementary - Often found on a straight line - If one angle is x°, its supplement is (180 - x)°

    Vertical Angles - Angles opposite each other when two lines intersect - Vertical angles are always equal - Important for understanding geometry diagrams

    Adjacent Angles - Angles that share a common vertex and a common side - Found frequently in geometric figures - May or may not be equal

    Angle Measurement

    Degrees: The most common unit for measuring angles in basic trigonometry - One complete rotation = 360° - A right angle = 90° - Degrees can be subdivided into minutes (') and seconds (") - 1° = 60 minutes, 1 minute = 60 seconds

    Radians: An alternative unit used especially in advanced mathematics - One complete rotation = 2π radians - A right angle = π/2 radians - We'll use degrees primarily in this course, with brief introduction to radians

    Interior and Exterior Angles

    Interior Angles: The angles inside a triangle (the ones we usually focus on)

    Exterior Angles: Angles formed by extending one side of a triangle beyond a vertex - An exterior angle and its adjacent interior angle are supplementary (sum to 180°) - An exterior angle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles

    Worked Examples

    Example 1.1: Working with Complementary Angles

    Problem: Two complementary angles have measures where one angle is 20° more than the other. Find both angles.

    Solution:

    Let x = the smaller angle Then x + 20 = the larger angle

    Since they're complementary, they sum to 90°: \(\(x + (x + 20) = 90°\)\)

    \[2x + 20 = 90°\]
    \[2x = 70°\]
    \[x = 35°\]

    Answer: - Smaller angle: 35° - Larger angle: 55°

    Verification: 35° + 55° = 90° ✓

    Example 1.2: Identifying Supplementary Angles

    Problem: Two supplementary angles have a ratio of 2:3. Find both angles.

    Solution:

    Let 2x = the smaller angle Let 3x = the larger angle

    Since they're supplementary, they sum to 180°: \(\(2x + 3x = 180°\)\)

    \[5x = 180°\]
    \[x = 36°\]

    Answer: - Smaller angle: 2(36°) = 72° - Larger angle: 3(36°) = 108°

    Verification: 72° + 108° = 180° ✓

    Example 1.3: Using Vertical Angles

    Problem: Two lines intersect forming four angles. One angle measures 65°. What are the measures of the other three angles?

    Solution:

    When two lines intersect, they form two pairs of vertical angles: - Vertical angles are equal - Adjacent angles are supplementary (sum to 180°)

    If one angle is 65°: - Its vertical angle = 65° (vertical angles equal) - Each adjacent angle = 180° - 65° = 115° - The angle vertical to one of the 115° angles = 115°

    Answer: The four angles are 65°, 115°, 65°, and 115°

    Practice Problems

    Problem 1.1: Find the complement of each angle: 1. 25° 2. 45° 3. 72°

    Problem 1.2: Find the supplement of each angle: 1. 40° 2. 125° 3. 88°

    Problem 1.3: Two complementary angles are in the ratio 3:2. Find both angles.

    Problem 1.4: Three angles of a triangle measure 40°, 60°, and 80°. Are these angles correct? Explain.

    Problem 1.5: Determine if the following pairs of angles are complementary, supplementary, or neither: 1. 45° and 45° 2. 60° and 120° 3. 30° and 60°

    Section 1.2: Triangle Types and Properties

    What is a Triangle?

    A triangle is a polygon with three sides, three angles, and three vertices (corner points). Triangles are among the most fundamental shapes in geometry and serve as the foundation for trigonometry.

    Key Components of a Triangle: - Sides: The three line segments that form the triangle's boundary - Angles: The three angles formed where sides meet - Vertices: The three points where sides intersect

    Important Property: The sum of all three angles in any triangle equals 180 degrees.

    Types of Triangles

    Triangles are classified in two main ways: by their sides and by their angles.

    Classification by Sides

    Equilateral Triangle - All three sides have equal length - All three angles are equal (each measures 60°) - Perfectly balanced and symmetrical

    Isosceles Triangle - Two sides have equal length - Two angles are equal (the angles opposite the equal sides) - Has a line of symmetry

    Scalene Triangle - All three sides have different lengths - All three angles are different sizes - Asymmetrical shape

    Classification by Angles

    Acute Triangle - All three angles are less than 90° - Sometimes called "sharp" triangles - Often appears pointed or well-balanced

    Right Triangle - One angle is exactly 90° (a right angle, shown with a small square) - The other two angles are acute (less than 90°) - Sides have special relationships (Pythagorean theorem) - Most important for trigonometry!

    Obtuse Triangle - One angle is greater than 90° (obtuse) - The other two angles are acute - Often appears "flat" or spread out

    Important Geometric Properties

    Angle Sum Property: The sum of all three interior angles in any triangle is always 180°.

    If a triangle has angles A, B, and C, then: \(\(A + B + C = 180°\)\)

    Exterior Angle Property: An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

    Vertex Relationships: Understanding how angles and sides relate at vertices is crucial for later work with trigonometric ratios.

    Worked Examples

    Example 1.4: Identifying Triangle Types

    Problem: Classify each triangle: 1. A triangle with sides 5 cm, 5 cm, and 7 cm 2. A triangle with angles 45°, 45°, and 90° 3. A triangle with angles 30°, 60°, and 90°

    Solution:

    1. First triangle (5 cm, 5 cm, 7 cm)
    2. Two sides are equal (5 cm each)
    3. Classification by sides: Isosceles triangle

    4. To classify by angles, we'd need to calculate them using more advanced methods

    5. Second triangle (45°, 45°, 90°)

    6. One angle is exactly 90°
    7. Classification by angles: Right triangle
    8. The two equal 45° angles suggest this is also isosceles (since equal angles opposite equal sides)

    9. Full classification: Isosceles right triangle

    10. Third triangle (30°, 60°, 90°)

    11. One angle is exactly 90°
    12. Classification by angles: Right triangle
    13. This is a special right triangle (30-60-90)
    14. All three sides have different lengths
    15. Full classification: Scalene right triangle

    Example 1.5: Using the Angle Sum Property

    Problem: In a triangle, one angle measures 50°, another measures 65°. What is the measure of the third angle?

    Solution:

    Using the angle sum property: \(\(A + B + C = 180°\)\)

    Substituting known values: \(\(50° + 65° + C = 180°\)\)

    \[115° + C = 180°\]
    \[C = 180° - 115°\]
    \[C = 65°\]

    Answer: The third angle measures 65°.

    Note: Since two angles are equal (65° each), this is an isosceles triangle.

    Example 1.6: Finding an Exterior Angle

    Problem: A triangle has interior angles of 40°, 70°, and 70°. If you extend one of the sides forming the 40° angle, what is the measure of the exterior angle formed?

    Solution:

    Using the exterior angle property: An exterior angle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

    The two non-adjacent angles (the ones not at the vertex where the exterior angle is formed) are 70° and 70°.

    Exterior angle = 70° + 70° = 140°

    Verification: The exterior angle and its adjacent interior angle should sum to 180°: - 140° + 40° = 180° ✓

    Practice Problems

    Problem 1.6: Name the type of triangle (by sides) that has: 1. All sides of different lengths 2. Two sides of equal length 3. All three sides of equal length

    Problem 1.7: A triangle has angles measuring 55°, 60°, and x°. Find the value of x.

    Problem 1.8: In an isosceles triangle, two angles measure 70° each. What is the third angle?

    Problem 1.9: Classify each triangle by angles: 1. Angles: 30°, 60°, 90° 2. Angles: 50°, 50°, 80° 3. Angles: 100°, 40°, 40°

    Problem 1.10: In a right triangle, one acute angle measures 35°. What is the measure of the other acute angle?

    Section 1.3: Chapter Summary and Review

    Key Concepts Reviewed

    Triangle Classification - By sides: Equilateral, Isosceles, Scalene - By angles: Acute, Right, Obtuse - A triangle can be classified both ways

    Important Triangle Property - Sum of interior angles = 180°

    Angle Types and Relationships - Complementary angles sum to 90° - Supplementary angles sum to 180° - Vertical angles are equal - Adjacent angles share a vertex and side

    Exterior Angles - Exterior angle = sum of two non-adjacent interior angles - Exterior angle + adjacent interior angle = 180°

    Why This Matters for Trigonometry

    Understanding these geometric foundations is essential because:

    1. Right triangles are central to trigonometry - We'll use the properties of right triangles throughout this course

    2. Angle relationships help us solve problems - Knowing complementary angles, for example, helps us understand trigonometric ratios

    3. Vocabulary and concepts are referenced constantly - Terms like "adjacent side," "opposite side," and "vertex" appear throughout trigonometry

    4. Geometric visualization supports understanding - Many trigonometric concepts are easier to understand when you can visualize the geometric relationships

    Connections to Trigonometry

    These geometric concepts directly connect to trigonometry:

    • Right triangles: The foundation for sine, cosine, and tangent ratios
    • Complementary angles: In a right triangle, the two acute angles are always complementary
    • Angle measurement: Degrees are used to specify angles in trigonometric ratios
    • Sides and vertices: Understanding "opposite," "adjacent," and "hypotenuse" requires geometric understanding
